\markboth{Abstract}{Abstract}

This Bachelor's Thesis studies electrocardiogram analysis when the ECG is treated as an image and only a small number of labeled examples is available. The central question is not only whether a CNN can classify traces, but when it is worth training a task-specific visual model and when vision-language models with in-context learning may become a better option. The clinical use case is Brugada syndrome, framed strictly as triage: a model alert should prioritize expert review, never confirm or rule out the diagnosis by itself.

The clinical workflow is essential for this framing. In routine practice, a suspicious Brugada pattern requires expert ECG review, assessment of symptoms and family history, possibly recording right precordial leads in higher intercostal spaces, and, in selected patients with persistent suspicion and a non-diagnostic baseline ECG, a sodium-channel blocker challenge. Such a test can unmask a type 1 pattern, but it must be interpreted together with the clinical context and after excluding phenocopies. Therefore, the system studied here is a prioritization filter, not an autonomous diagnostic classifier.

The work combines two domains. The first one is a controlled QRS/ST morphology simulator that renders V1, V2 and V3 images and assigns labels from observable rules. The second one is the Brugada HUCA dataset, processed from real clinical recordings into right precordial lead windows with a binary target: confirmed Brugada case versus control. Evaluation is patient-level leave-one-out cross-validation with reduced budgets \(K=\{2,4,8,16,32\}\) and three seeds per budget.

The completed experimental phase corresponds to the CNN branch. ResNet18 performs clearly on the simulator: mean balanced accuracy rises from 0.625 at \(K=2\) to 0.848 at \(K=32\), with sensitivity 0.883, specificity 0.812, F1 0.673 and ROC AUC 0.857. On Brugada HUCA, however, performance drops close to chance. The best baseline balanced accuracy is obtained at \(K=16\), 0.516, while \(K=32\) reaches 0.499. Sensitivity remains moderate, but specificity is low.

A domain adaptation branch is also reviewed and executed. The tested methods are self-supervised learning, CORAL, MMD and DANN, evaluated at \(K=16\) and \(K=32\). The best real-data result is obtained by MMD at \(K=32\), with balanced accuracy 0.549, F1 0.498, sensitivity 0.649 and specificity 0.448. The improvement is measurable but insufficient: even after domain adaptation, the CNN branch is not robust on real Brugada data under such limited supervision.

This negative evidence is a central contribution. The CNN branch establishes a reproducible baseline and exposes the practical limit of training task-specific visual models in the few-data regime. On top of that baseline, the next phase is defined as a comparison against open vision-language models, such as Gemma 4 and MedGemma, using the same \(K\) budgets as in-context demonstrations. The thesis documents the VLM/ICL pipeline, prompts, JSON validation and controls, but reports no VLM metrics because complete audited inference artifacts are not yet available.
