\markboth{Abstract}{Abstract}

This Bachelor's Thesis addresses automatic ECG analysis when the available input is not the original digital signal but an image of the trace. This setting is common in retrospective reviews and clinical workflows where ECGs are exchanged as scanned paper, PDF files or screenshots embedded in electronic health records. The problem is studied under a particularly restrictive condition: few labelled patients and a triage task related to suspected Brugada syndrome. The system is not proposed as an automatic diagnostic tool, but as a research prototype for prioritizing traces that should be reviewed by specialists.

The thesis compares two ways of dealing with this scarcity of labelled data. Both approaches receive ECGs as images, are evaluated at patient level and use the same example-set sizes \(K=\{2,4,8,16,32\}\). The evaluation combines two sources. The first is a QRS/ST morphological simulator that generates V1, V2 and V3 images and assigns labels using rules on trace morphology. The second is a real dataset provided by the Hospital Universitario Central de Asturias, with 317 patients and 951 images classified as Brugada case or control.

The first approach is supervised. A ResNet18 uses labelled simulator patients to update its weights and learn three morphological findings. A supervised real-to-real CNN is not trained with the dataset provided by HUCA because the clinical cohort does not contain independent image-level annotations for RBBB, ST elevation and T-wave inversion. The real evaluation therefore measures transfer from the simulator to the clinical cohort. On the simulator, the CNN reaches balanced accuracy 0.848 at \(K=32\), with sensitivity 0.883 and specificity 0.812. On the real dataset, baseline performance remains close to chance, with a best balanced accuracy of 0.516 at \(K=16\). Domain adaptation improves this modestly to 0.549 with MMD at \(K=32\).

The second approach is multimodal. Gemma~4 is used as a vision-language model with in-context learning: the labelled examples do not modify the model weights, but are inserted into the prompt as demonstrations before the image to be classified. Zero-shot, normal ICL and balanced ICL are compared. On the simulator, demonstrations clearly improve over zero-shot and reach balanced accuracy 0.754 with balanced context at \(K=8\). On real ECGs, the best point is around 0.522 at \(K=16\). Balanced context increases sensitivity to 0.713, but specificity falls to 0.332.

Overall, the work shows that ECG-as-image analysis contains useful signal in a controlled domain, but transfer to real Brugada data remains limited with few examples. The CNN benefits more clearly from additional data on the simulator. In-context learning does not require retraining the model for each new dataset and is easier to move between evaluation settings, although in these experiments it increases sensitivity at the cost of lower specificity. When the VLM receives simulator context and is queried on the ECGs provided by HUCA, performance collapses back towards the negative class, reinforcing the difficulty of the synthetic-to-real gap. Neither approach is yet robust enough for reliable clinical triage. The main contribution of the thesis is to characterize this limitation through a reproducible protocol and to show that further progress will require more positive cases, clinical morphological annotations and external validation.

\textbf{Keywords:} ECG as image, Brugada syndrome, few labelled data, supervised learning, convolutional neural networks, vision-language models, in-context learning, clinical triage.
